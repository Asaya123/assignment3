Trevi Fountain

The **Trevi Fountain**, completed in 1762, is a baroque masterpiece and one of the most famous fountains in the world. Located in the heart of Rome, it stands as a symbol of the city's rich artistic and architectural heritage.

Designed by **Nicola Salvi**, the fountain was commissioned by Pope Clement XII. It features an elaborate design with a central figure of **Neptune**, the god of the sea, flanked by two Tritons. The fountain's dramatic sculptural elements and intricate carvings are a testament to the opulence of the late baroque period.

The Trevi Fountain is also famous for the tradition of throwing coins into its waters. According to legend, doing so ensures a return to Rome in the future. This ritual has made the fountain not only a stunning piece of art but also a popular tourist attraction.

The fountain is supplied by the **Vergine Aqueduct**, which dates back to 19 BCE, making it one of the oldest working aqueducts in Rome. The Trevi Fountain remains a beloved monument, admired by millions who visit it each year to marvel at its beauty and partake in the time-honored tradition of coin throwing.

Visiting the **Trevi Fountain** is a must for anyone traveling to Rome. The grandeur of its design and the peaceful sound of its waters offer a memorable experience for all who come to see it.
